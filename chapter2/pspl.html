Review from Chapter 1: the deflection of light

Light is bent (aka deflected) by the presence of mass:

$$\alpha = \frac{4GM}{bc^2}$$
  • α = deflection angle
  • M = mass of the lens
  • b = impact parameter (distance of closest approach)
  • G = Newton's gravitational constant
  • c = speed of light

More massive lenses (i.e., larger M) = larger deflection angle α.

Light passing closer to the lens M (i.e., smaller b) = larger deflection angle α.

Note somewhere that all these positions are angles. Also be clear when a figure is a front view, vs. side view.

Review from Chapter 1: the lens equation

The lens equation describes where the light looks like it is coming from:

$$\theta^2 - \theta \theta_S - \theta_E^2 = 0, \quad \theta_E \equiv \sqrt{\frac{4GM}{c^2} \left( \frac{1}{D_L} - \frac{1}{D_S} \right)}$$
  • θ = image position
  • θS = background source position
  • θE = Einstein radius (characteristic angular scale: depends on lens mass M, observer's distance to the lens DL, source DS)

Connecting the lens equation to magnification

Surface brightness is conserved in microlensing: things don't look brighter when you look at them with a magnifying glass.

Then, the ratio of the area of the images to the area of the source is equal to the amount of magnification.

$$A_1 = \frac{\theta_1 \, d\theta_1}{\theta_S \, d\theta_S}, A_2 = \frac{\theta_2 \, d\theta_2}{\theta_S \, d\theta_S}$$

where theta_1 is the + solution of the quadratic equation, and theta_2 is the - solution of the quadratic equation.

Since there are two images, the total magnification will be the sum of the individual magnifications, A = |A_1| + |A_2|.

Connecting the lens equation to magnification

We will skip the math (you can try this for yourself by differentiating the two solutions of the lens equation wrt θS), but if you calculate what A_1 and A_2 are, the answer you get is:

$$A(u) = \frac{u^2+2}{u\sqrt{u^2+4}}$$

where u is the distance of the source from the lens in units of the Einstein radius (dimensionless impact parameter)

$$u \equiv \theta_S/\theta_E$$

Complication: microlensing parallax

Earth orbits around the Sun: it is accelerating!

This can cause the otherwise symmetric lightcurve to not be symmetric: it can be a subtle asymmetry (common), or introduce a prominent wiggle on top of the light curve (rare). This is called microlensing parallax.

Note: Trigonometric parallax (often just called parallax) is the change in position of nearby sources relative to distant background stars, due to Earth orbiting the Sun.

Goal 2: How likely it is to see microlensing?

Microlensing optical depth

Microlensing optical depth

Definition: the probability that a star will lie within the Einstein radius of a foreground lens.

This depends on the source: remember that the Einstein radius depends on the source distance!

Important quantity because it tells you about the distribution of mass along a line of sight.

Note: in astronomy, optical depth typically means how transparent a material is to light (high optical depth = opaque, low optical depth = transparent). It has absolutely nothing to do with gravitational lensing.
